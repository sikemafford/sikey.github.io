April 19, 1775   The Battle of Lexington

While Paul Revere was on his moonlit ride to alert the countryside,  he was detained by British soldiers who were posted outside of town.   One placed a pistol to Revere's head saying that Revere may want to give up some reliable information or they would "blow [his] brains out."  Revere offered that the soldiers would be confronted by 500 militiamen soon.   Soon released after being deprived of his horse, Revere proceeded to Lexington on foot.   Meanwhile,  the news of approaching troops spread like wildfire throughout the countryside.  Church bells rang and shots were fired into the night--all to alert the militia that had been assembling all night at Lexington green.  Lexington green was a small, triangular section of land in the middle of town between forking roads--one of which led to the troop's destination: the Concord arsenal.    A drum steadily rolled while the militamen, many ordinary family people (fathers, sons, grandfathers),  came together.  At dawn, Revere was allowed to pass through milita troops just ahead of the British soldiers.   They listened to a dreadfully slow and terrifying march of the seasoned, professional soldiers toward them.   As the soldiers approached, the British troops saw what they would be confronting:  several dozen men--a fraction of the British numbers.   The militiamen were ordered by the British commander to disperse.  Greatly outnumbered, they began to do so.  The Britsh troops attempted to flank and contain them.  The "shot heard 'round the world" rang out--the British said it was the militiamen; militiamen said it was the British who fired first.    Eight militiamen died.  One British soldier was slightly wounded.    The British commander,  Pitcairn,'s horse was grazed by a bullet.

April 19, 1775   The Battle of Concord

As the British left Lexington and marched toward Concord, more militiamen began their march to intercept the troops. Upon seeing the superior numbers, the militiamen retreated to a nearby hilltop. The British entered Concord and began destroying munitions as well as forcing the reluctant locals to sell them victuals.  The militamen's alarm and fury were enflamed because many of the colonists believed that their homes had been set ablaze as well as their munitions destroyed. The militamen moved toward Conocord from the hilltop and confronted troops at North Bridge. Two colonists were killed. The British retreated to Concord to await reinforcements.  The signaling bells continued to toll and the militia numbers continued to grow.   Since the reinforcements were slow to arrive, the British retreated to Boston from whence they came. As they marched (and to their utter outrage), militia fire came guerrilla-style from behind objects rather than in the honorable, upright and open way to which the British were accustomed to being perforated with lead. In the end, the battle was an embarassment to the British and a triumph for the colonists. Britsh losses were 73; American were 49.

April 19, 1775    The Seige of Boston Begins...

After Concord, a period of the war began in which the British were contained in the city of Boston. It was know as the Seige of Boston. During this time Washington was given command of the Continental Army

May 10, 1775   Battle of Ticonderoga

The rebel army was in need of supplies during the Seige of Boston.  Fort Ticonderoga could offer those supplies and a prime strategic location for controlling the northern region. Benedict Arnold manage to convince Massachusetts authorities to allow him to lead men in the capture of the fort.   At the same time, Ethan Allen, leader of the Green Mountain boys of the New Hampshire Grants, was on the same mission.   Neither willing to give up the glory of capturing the fort, they agreed--after a bit of debate--to a co-command of the forces. They slipped into the fort under cover of night while the troops there slept.  It was quickly surrendered and the ample stores of cannon were seized and sent to the Seige of Boston.  This virtually bloodless capture was integral to eventual American control of Boston.

     

June 17, 1775   The Battle of Bunker Hill

  A sleeping colonial giant had been awoken. After Lexington and Concord, the tables were turned on the soldiers in Boston who had once so insouciantly dominated the city. It was the British troops who were now being subjected to the intimidation of a vaste numbers of militiamen. In the dead of night, the militia managed to create two fortifications overlooking the entrenched enemy in Boston at Bunker Hill and Breed's Hill, which gave them a strategic advantage. Three separate attacks were launched by the British until the colonists' supplies were exhausted and the British eventually took the fortification. The weary British soldiers did not pursue the retreating patriots. Though a loss for the patriots, the Battle of Bunker Hill was a tremendous morale booster for the patriots. They knew they could hold their own against the formidable enemy.

July 8, 1775   The Olive Branch Petition

Because the colonists had shown themselves as a force to be reckoned with,  Richard Penn and Arthur Lee (of the Continental Congress) felt that they had some leverage for a deal with the king.  After reviewing a forked-tongued offering first written by the pugnacious and eloquent Jefferson, the Congress settled on a kinder and gentler version by the more moderate John Dickinson. (Adams, ever the realist, sat out the affair and waited for the inevitable esclation of the conflict into a full-blown war.) The congress submitted a petition to King George, The Olive Branch Petition in the hopes of achieving some sort of peace.  The petition was openly intended to circumvent parliamentary authority and appeal directly to the king for some sort of reconciliation.

August 23, 1775   The Declaration of Rebellion

  In an age of endless, long-winded legal proclamation, the king's answer to the unread Olive Branch Petition was relatively curt. The Declaration of Rebellion ordered all loyal officials and subjects to suppress the apparent rebellion.

December 5, 1775   Henry Knox Begins Transporting 59 Cannon from Fort Ticoderoga...

War presents endless situations that require all the innovation and resourcefulness that humans can muster.  On orders from General Washington, the young bookseller, Henry Knox, was given the logistically daunting task of transporting cannon 300 miles from captured Fort Ticonderoga to Boston to checkmate the British troops still occupying Boston. For two months he and his men and many oxen dragged 60 tons of cannon (munitions, etc.) weighing more than a Cadillac Escalade over land, water, and up and down hill after endless hill. The going was tough.  His men at one point refused to go on, but Knox, evidently the consumate young leader, persuaded them to continue...

     

December 31, 1775   The Battle of Quebec

Under the gouty General Montgomery and the brilliant,  ambitious and later despicably traitorous Colonel Benedict Arnold,  the Americans launched an attack on the 14th colony: Quebec. After a horrendous march through 350 miles of winter wilderness,  Arnold’s men (the ones who did not have the indecency to desert--or die) arrived half-starved at Quebec and eventually met up with Montgomery and his troops.  The two launched their two-pronged attack as a winter storm sent snow sideways into the troops’ faces.  The Americans fought to no avail.   Quebec would not so easily be taken. Montgomery died during the siege.  Arnold was wounded.  American losses were heavy (500); British were minimal (20).   Arnold eventually withdrew his forces.
